Tipi

Tipi's use Lodge Pole Pine wood as their structure. Lewis and Clark had named the trees Lodge Pole pine because of their encounter upon the First Nations peoples.

Blackfoot tipi's base are made up of four tipi poles tied together. They tie the poles together while the poles are lying flat on the ground. Once they are all tied tightly together they leave the rope to dangle in the middle of the poles so they can have one person to hold the rope and act as anchor. The others will walk the tipi poles up and then extend the poles outwards, almost to look like a tripod. (two poles in the front, two in the back).

Tipi doors always face East. This is to welcome in the Blackfoot peoples creator, Natosi-the Sun. It also, represented a new begining.

Tipi Rings

Back in the Dog Days, the Blackfoot people would use stones/rocks to hold down their tipi's to act as pegs. There are tipi rings found throughout the plains area. Today, people may refer to tipi rings as medicine wheels.
